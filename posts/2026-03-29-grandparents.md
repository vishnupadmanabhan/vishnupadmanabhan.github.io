Sardine, mackerel, sometimes tuna. Six or seven pieces deep into a meal, my grandmother still pushing more onto my plate, my mother watching from across the table with the particular expression of someone who knows she has already lost.

Summer vacations were always at grandma's. She wouldn't have it any other way. She needed us the next day of school closing. We used to stay for over a month, sometimes multiple times a year. Becuase being at your grandparents was something to look forward to. Food was one of the reasons.

When I think about here, I understand that grandchidren for her were different from her own children. There was a definite warmer tone to her actions to us. She was very caring towards her children, but she was extra warm towards us. Since it was how we knew here, did not feel like anything special back then. But now when I look back, it seems like there was a difference in the way we were treated.

III. What She Was Like With Her What you know, or can infer, about how she raised your mother. Keep it close to what you actually have — don't invent. Even a fragment or a texture is enough.

IV. The Grandmother Hypothesis The science, short. Maternal grandmothers invest most in grandchildren because the line of genetic certainty runs cleanest through them. Wear it lightly — one paragraph, no more. State it, let it sit.

V. Love Without the Project The psychological explanation, and probably the one that feels most true. Parenting is a project — you are responsible for making a person into someone. Grandparenting carries none of that weight. The love arrives without the fear attached. This is where the analytical muscle does its real work.

VI. What My Mother Knew A fragment, a glimpse of how your mother experienced or understood this. Even something small. This is the section that returns your grandmother from theory back to person.

VII. No Answer Required You don't need to understand it fully. You've chosen not to. What you have instead is the image — sardine, mackerel, your grandmother's hand already moving toward the plate — which you now hold differently than you did as a child, but are not trying to solve.
